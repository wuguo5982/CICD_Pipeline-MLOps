CMS FWA review principle
Fraud, waste, and abuse review should combine claim facts, provider history, coding policy, medical necessity documentation, and human compliance review. AI output is a screening aid, not a final fraud determination.

Medical necessity
Services must be reasonable and necessary for diagnosis or treatment. When documentation does not support medical necessity, the claim should be escalated for manual review rather than automatically denied by an AI system.

Upcoding
High-complexity evaluation and management codes require documentation supporting complexity, time, decision-making, and patient condition. Upcoding risk increases when a high-complexity code is paired with routine diagnosis context.

Duplicate or rapid repeat billing
Multiple claims for the same patient, provider, and service in a short time window may indicate duplicate billing, fragmented billing, or unnecessary utilization.

Provider outlier review
Providers with unusual utilization, high-cost patterns, or high peer percentile should be reviewed using peer benchmarks, specialty context, and documentation evidence.

DME billing support
Durable medical equipment claims require diagnosis support, physician order, and proof that the equipment is reasonable and necessary.

Ambulance transport
Non-emergency ambulance transport requires medical necessity, origin and destination support, and evidence that other transportation was contraindicated.

Unclassified drug codes
Unclassified drug codes require supporting documentation, pricing evidence, dosage details, and clinical rationale.

Human review escalation
High-risk model scores, anomaly flags, unsupported evidence, safety risks, or weak retrieval should trigger human review. The system should abstain from final accusations.

RAG grounding
Generated explanations should cite retrieved policy, audit rules, benchmark tables, or clinical-note evidence. Unsupported high-risk claims should be removed or replaced with uncertainty language.
